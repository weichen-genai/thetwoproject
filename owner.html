Book project

The Owner Book

A business novel about what happens when AI changes tasks but nobody owns the workflow.

The book turns the T-W-O thesis into narrative form: meetings, incentives, uncertainty, and the practical struggle of deciding who is responsible for redesigning work.

Read the preview

Premise

A story about organizational change disguised as an AI initiative.

The Owner Book follows leaders and operators who initially treat AI as a tooling problem. What emerges instead is a deeper operating question: when tasks move, who redesigns the workflow around them, absorbs the risk, and makes the change stick?

Why this book

The argument becomes clearer when it is lived through people, pressure, and decisions.

The book format makes room for ambiguity, resistance, and misalignment. Instead of presenting a management model in abstract terms, it shows how workflow redesign collides with status, incentives, and operational reality.

What readers get

A practical way to think about AI transformation, but through narrative tension rather than framework-first exposition.

Inside the book

A concise book structure with room for story, ideas, and operating detail.

Part I: The false start

Teams chase AI speed without changing the surrounding workflow, and the limits of that approach become visible.

Part II: The workflow problem

The central conflict sharpens around validation, exceptions, sequencing, and the cost of unclear ownership.

Part III: The owner question

Leadership has to decide who can redesign the way work moves, not just who can deploy another model or tool.

Part IV: A new operating logic

The organization begins to see AI as a redesign challenge across tasks, workflows, and structure.

Who it is for

Leaders, operators, builders, and researchers trying to understand why AI changes are easy to demo but hard to institutionalize.

How it connects to T-W-O

The book is the narrative companion to the framework, focusing on workflow ownership as the missing middle of AI transformation.
